Using Twitter‚Äôs streaming API, I collected ~1.8 million tweets (over a week last month) that were geolocated within the USA. Of those, ~410k tweets contained at least one emoji. From there, I split up the tweets based on the state they came from and then tallied up all of the emojis.

A couple initial thoughts: 1) the emoji dictionary is expansive‚Äîthere were 1,067 different emojis used across all of the tweets. 2) The ‚Äùface with tears of joy‚Äù emoji (üòÇ) is crazy popular. If you simply plotted the most commonly used emoji for each state (which I did at first), it‚Äôs a pretty boring and repetitive map with 49 üòÇs out of 51 (including DC). To overcome the monotony, we can compute the ‚Äúterm frequency‚Äìinverse document frequency‚Äù (tf‚Äìidf for short) for each emoji. This is a numerical technique to determine words (emojis) that are significant to a document (tweets within a state) within a larger corpus (all USA tweets). As explained by Wikipedia, ‚Äùthe tf-idf value increases proportionally to the number of times a word appears in the document, but is often offset by the frequency of the word in the corpus, which helps to adjust for the fact that some words appear more frequently in general.‚Äù

With emojis ranked by tf-idf, the maps (top 1, top 4, also see image above) are a lot more interesting.
